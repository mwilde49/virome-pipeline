## 1. Executive Summary and Project Context

### 1.1 Scientific Motivation

The human dorsal root ganglion (DRG) is the primary sensory ganglia of the peripheral nervous system. DRG neurons are the natural latency reservoir for neurotropic herpesviruses including herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1), varicella-zoster virus (VZV), and Epstein-Barr virus (EBV). The virome of human DRG tissue has been largely uncharacterized due to the difficulty of obtaining fresh post-mortem tissue and the challenge of distinguishing genuine viral signal from the abundant noise generated by k-mer-based metagenomic classifiers operating on bulk RNA-seq data.

This pipeline addresses that gap. It is designed specifically for systematic profiling of viral RNA expression in paired-end bulk RNA-seq data generated from human DRG and related tissue types (muscle, spinal cord). The tissue of origin is central to every design decision: the artifacts, the filtering parameters, the confidence thresholds, and the interpretation framework are all calibrated to DRG-specific biology.

### 1.2 Clinical Context

Two primary cohort applications drive pipeline development:

**Paper 1 (non-PD DRG cohort, n=21):** Systematic baseline characterization of the DRG virome in non-Parkinson's disease subjects. Primary finding: Tier 1 = 0 confirmed viral detections across all samples after dual-database validation. This establishes the noise floor and false positive landscape of the method.

**Paper 2 (Parkinson's disease DRG cohort, n=20, 2026):** Sequenced by Psomagen (order AN00028264). 14 PD patients (PD2-PD20, non-contiguous) and 6 controls (023-028). First Tier 1 detection in the program's history: HSV-1 detected in sample PD19 (46 reads, 1.89 RPM), confirmed by dual-database comparison. This detection, and the framework required to validate it with confidence, defines the current state of the art for this pipeline.

### 1.3 Key Technical Challenge: Signal-to-Noise in k-mer Classification

Bulk RNA-seq is not a purpose-built viromics assay. Total RNA libraries contain overwhelming host signal (~99.9% or more of reads are human). The k-mer-based classifier Kraken2 can generate false positive viral detections through several mechanisms:

- **LCA rollup:** Reads with k-mers shared across a genus or family are assigned to the lowest common ancestor, inflating parent-node counts and generating species-level detections that may not reflect true viral presence.
- **Database imbalance:** When a viral database contains reference sequences for closely related non-human viruses but not the human-tropic ortholog, human viral reads assign to the non-human taxon via LCA (documented example: human CMV → baboon CMV proxy).
- **Tissue-specific k-mer cross-mapping:** DRG neurons express a unique transcriptome (neuronal ion channels, neuropeptides, lncRNAs) with k-mer overlaps to environmental metagenome-derived virus sequences that have no vertebrate host range. These produce DRG-exclusive signals absent from muscle tissue, simulating biological specificity but reflecting artifact (confirmed: Gihfavirus pelohabitans, Kinglevirus lutadaptatum).
- **Index hopping:** Low-level contamination from adjacent wells during multiplexed sequencing creates sample-level detections at read counts that are suspicious precisely because they appear in all samples at similar levels (confirmed: Orthohantavirus oxbowense / Oxbow virus).
- **Reagent contamination:** Insect baculoviruses and bacteriophages from sequencing kit components produce consistent detections across all libraries regardless of tissue of origin.

The pipeline's filtering framework — dual-database tiering, curated artifact exclusion, empirically validated minimum read thresholds, and optional BLAST verification — exists to systematically address each of these failure modes.

## 3. Step 1: FASTQC

### 3.1 Purpose and Configuration

FastQC is run on raw reads prior to trimming to establish baseline quality metrics. It runs post-trimming as part of MultiQC aggregation. Resource allocation: 4 CPUs, 8 GB memory, 2-hour wall time.

### 3.2 Metrics and Interpretation for DRG RNA-seq

**Per-base sequence quality:** Expect Phred ≥ 30 across positions 1-140 for standard Illumina RNA-seq. Degradation at position 141-150 is common and acceptable; TRAILING:3 in TRIMMOMATIC will handle it. A drop before position 50 may indicate library quality issues specific to post-mortem tissue processing.

**Adapter content:** Expect TruSeq3-PE adapter sequences in the final ~10-15 positions of reads from shorter-insert libraries. Any adapter detected before position 120 indicates unusually short inserts and warrants inspection of library QC metrics.

**Sequence duplication levels:** RNA-seq libraries typically show 20-60% duplication depending on transcript abundance. DRG samples show elevated duplication for highly expressed neuronal genes (SCN9A, TRPV1, CALCA). High duplication (>80%) in a specific sample may indicate low library complexity from degraded RNA; check RIN value.

**Per-sequence GC content:** Deviations from the expected bell curve (mean ~50% GC) indicate contamination or adapter dimer issues. A secondary peak at low GC in a DRG sample may reflect viral RNA contamination if confirmed by downstream steps. Anomalously high GC (>60%) can indicate rRNA contamination.

**Overrepresented sequences:** BLAST-identify any flagged sequences. In DRG libraries, overrepresented sequences may include:
- rRNA fragments (indicates rRNA depletion failure; if >10% of reads, consider rRNA depletion QC)
- Adapter sequences (handled by TRIMMOMATIC)
- Poly-A tails (3' terminal homopolymers from RNA degradation; TRAILING:3 handles end-terminal issues)

**Sequence length distribution:** Post-trimming should be 36-150 bp. A spike at MINLEN:36 (the minimum) suggests many short reads being kept at the threshold; consider whether reducing MINLEN would reduce noise or whether increasing MINLEN would improve specificity.

## 4. Step 2: TRIMMOMATIC

### 4.1 Command and Parameters

```
trimmomatic PE \
  -threads {cpus} \
  -phred33 \
  ILLUMINACLIP:{adapters}:2:30:10:8:true \
  {HEADCROP:{trim_headcrop}} \
  LEADING:{trim_leading} \
  TRAILING:{trim_trailing} \
  SLIDINGWINDOW:{trim_slidingwindow} \
  MINLEN:{trim_minlen}
```

Default parameter set (as used in all production runs to date):

| Parameter | Default Value |
|---|---|
| Adapter file | TruSeq3-PE (assets/NexteraPE-PE.fa also available) |
| HEADCROP | 0 (disabled) |
| LEADING | 3 |
| TRAILING | 3 |
| SLIDINGWINDOW | 4:15 |
| MINLEN | 36 |

Resource allocation: 8 CPUs, 16 GB memory, 12-hour wall time (time limit accounts for outlier sample donor1-L5 at 174M reads).

### 4.2 ILLUMINACLIP Parameters: Detailed Rationale

The ILLUMINACLIP argument `2:30:10:8:true` unpacks as:

- **seedMismatches = 2:** Maximum number of mismatches allowed in the 16-base seed alignment when searching for adapter sequences. A value of 2 provides robust detection while avoiding spurious matches to non-adapter sequence.
- **palindromeClipThreshold = 30:** Score threshold for palindrome alignment (detecting adapter read-through in paired reads). Higher values are more permissive. At 30, adapters are detected when the palindromic structure contributes a log-likelihood score ≥ 30. This is the most important parameter for detecting short-insert adapter contamination common in DRG libraries.
- **simpleClipThreshold = 10:** Score threshold for simple sequence alignment of the adapter against the read. At 10, approximately 17 matching bases are required for a clip decision.
- **minAdapterLength = 8:** Minimum adapter fragment length to clip. Prevents removal of short coincidental matches that are not genuine adapters.
- **keepBothReads = true:** When one read in a pair is shorter than MINLEN after clipping, both reads in the pair are retained in the paired output rather than discarding both. Critical for paired-end Kraken2 operation: Kraken2 uses `--paired` mode and requires both mates. Discarding entire pairs due to one short mate would reduce viral detection sensitivity.

### 4.3 LEADING and TRAILING

`LEADING:3` removes bases from the 5' end of each read with quality below Phred 3. This quality threshold is deliberately conservative — it catches only the most egregious low-quality terminal bases (essentially N calls) without over-trimming. More aggressive LEADING values (e.g., LEADING:20) would remove legitimate 5' sequence from reads that begin with moderate quality.

`TRAILING:3` applies the same logic to the 3' end. The value 3 is appropriate for the bulk of reads where quality degrades modestly toward the 3' terminus. Illumina's standard degradation pattern rarely produces quality below 3 except at the final 1-2 positions. For samples with significant 3' quality degradation (visible in FastQC per-base quality plot), increasing to TRAILING:10 or TRAILING:15 is appropriate, with the tradeoff of shorter mean read length going into STAR.

### 4.4 SLIDINGWINDOW

`SLIDINGWINDOW:4:15` scans with a 4-base sliding window and clips when the average Phred quality within the window drops below 15. This is the primary mechanism for handling 3' quality degradation that TRAILING:3 misses. A window of 4 provides smoothing across adjacent bases while remaining sensitive enough to detect genuine quality drops. A threshold of 15 is moderately permissive; increasing to 4:20 increases specificity at the cost of shorter reads.

The window clips from the 5' end of the degraded region, discarding everything downstream. After sliding window clipping, MINLEN filters reads below the threshold length.

### 4.5 MINLEN:36

36 bases is the minimum read length retained after trimming. This value was chosen as a balance between:

- **Kraken2 k-mer requirement:** Kraken2 uses 35-mers by default. A read shorter than 35 bases cannot generate a k-mer. At MINLEN:36, every retained read can produce at least one k-mer.
- **Specificity:** Reads shorter than 36 bases have higher probability of spurious k-mer matches to unrelated taxa simply due to short sequence length.
- **Sensitivity:** Lowering MINLEN to 20-30 retains more reads from degraded or short-insert libraries but at cost of classification noise.

**When to change MINLEN:**
- Decrease to 20-25 for ultra-degraded post-mortem tissue RNA with confirmed short fragment length distribution.
- Increase to 50-75 for targeted viral detection studies where specificity is paramount and library quality is high.
- Never decrease below 35 — reads shorter than one k-mer add only noise.

### 4.6 HEADCROP

`HEADCROP:0` (disabled by default). HEADCROP removes a fixed number of bases from the 5' end of every read regardless of quality.

**When to enable HEADCROP:**
- **Cycle-1 base call artifacts:** Some Illumina configurations produce systematically low-quality or biased base calls in the first 1-5 cycles, visible as a spike in FastQC per-base quality at position 1-5. HEADCROP:5 cleanly removes this artifact.
- **NextSeq / NovaSeq polyG artifact:** Two-color chemistry instruments (NextSeq 500/550, NextSeq 2000, NovaSeq 6000) produce polyG tails (G is encoded as signal-absent in two-color chemistry) when a cluster loses signal. These appear as GGGGG... at 3' ends and are handled by TRAILING, not HEADCROP. However, if the instrument was operated in a configuration that introduces polyG at the 5' end (rare, but observed with certain library prep protocols), HEADCROP:5-10 is appropriate.
- **Variable-composition 5' primers:** Some library prep protocols (e.g., random hexamer priming for rRNA-depleted libraries) introduce composition bias at positions 1-6 due to non-uniform hexamer binding. HEADCROP:6 eliminates this without affecting downstream analysis.

### 4.7 Trade-off Summary: Aggressive vs. Conservative Trimming

| Strategy | Parameters | Effect on Viromics |
|---|---|---|
| Conservative (current) | LEADING:3, TRAILING:3, SLIDINGWINDOW:4:15, MINLEN:36 | Maximum read retention; highest sensitivity for low-abundance viral RNA |
| Moderate | LEADING:10, TRAILING:10, SLIDINGWINDOW:4:20, MINLEN:50 | Reduced noise from low-quality bases; may lose reads from divergent or low-abundance viruses |
| Aggressive | LEADING:20, TRAILING:20, SLIDINGWINDOW:4:25, MINLEN:75 | Maximum specificity; appropriate for validating known high-abundance detections; not appropriate for discovery |

The current conservative settings are correct for the discovery phase of DRG viromics. Aggressive trimming is appropriate for validation runs where the biological question is confirmation of a known detection (e.g., HSV-1 in PD19).

## 5. Step 3: STAR Host Removal — The Most Consequential Step

### 5.1 Overview and Purpose

STAR host removal is the most consequential step in the pipeline because it determines the composition of the read pool that reaches Kraken2. Any human read that escapes host removal will be subject to k-mer classification — and a fraction will misclassify as viral due to shared k-mer content between human genes and viral sequences. Conversely, any genuinely viral read that STAR incorrectly maps to the human genome will be permanently lost from the classification pool. The design decisions in this step represent a fundamental specificity-sensitivity tradeoff.

### 5.2 STAR Command Parameters

```
STAR \
  --runThreadN {cpus} \
  --genomeDir {star_index} \
  --readFilesIn r1 r2 \
  --readFilesCommand zcat \
  --outSAMtype BAM SortedByCoordinate \
  --outSAMattributes NH HI AS NM \
  --outFilterMultimapNmax 1 \
  --outFileNamePrefix {id}_ \
  --outReadsUnmapped Fastx \
  --runMode alignReads \
  {star_extra_args}
```

Resource allocation: 16 CPUs, 64 GB memory, 24-hour wall time. `stageInMode = 'symlink'` — the GRCh38 STAR index is 33 GB and must never be copied.

### 5.3 The Critical Parameter: --outFilterMultimapNmax 1

This is the single most important STAR parameter for viromics applications. It controls which aligned reads are reported in the output BAM — and by extension, which reads are treated as "mapped to human" and excluded from the Kraken2 input.

**--outFilterMultimapNmax 1 (current setting):** Only reads that align uniquely to the human genome (exactly one alignment site) are written to the BAM file and thus excluded from the Kraken2 input. Reads that could align to 2 or more human loci are considered "multimapping" and are emitted as unmapped reads — they pass to Kraken2.

**Biological rationale for this choice:**
- Viral reads that share homology with human sequences (e.g., HERV-K, which is integrated into the human genome and is genuinely part of the human genome as well as being transcribed from endogenous loci) would be removed by more aggressive settings.
- Many integrated proviruses and endogenous retroelements have close k-mer matches to both the human reference genome and exogenous viral databases. Multi-mapper status is the appropriate indicator that a read's genomic origin is ambiguous, and such reads should be evaluated by the viral classifier rather than presumed human.
- At --outFilterMultimapNmax 1, STAR preserves detection sensitivity for endogenous viral elements (EVEs), retroviruses, and divergent viruses with partial human homology.

**Effect of --outFilterMultimapNmax 10:** Reads that align to up to 10 locations in the human genome are removed. This would capture borderline reads with homology to both human and viral sequences, including:
- HERV-K: substantially reduces detected HERV-K RPM (HERV-K reads that multi-map to both integrated proviral loci and to exogenous retrovirus references would be removed)
- HSV-1 reads aligning near human herpesvirus conserved elements: minimal effect expected, as HSV-1 shares negligible sequence identity with human genes, but conserved herpesvirus gene families (DNA polymerase, capsid proteins) could have 2-3 alignments across the human genome at repetitive loci
- Setting of 10 is appropriate if HERV-K is not a biological question of interest and reducing its noise contribution is desired

**Effect of --outFilterMultimapNmax 0:** Removes ALL reads that align to the human genome, including those with zero secondary alignments (reads that map uniquely). This is equivalent to removing every read with any human homology, which is far too aggressive for a viromics assay. It would remove:
- All reads from endogenous viral elements (which are part of the human genome)
- Reads from viruses with any degree of human genome homology
- Standard STAR behavior with value 0 is undefined; the parameter should never be set below 1 in this application

**Recommendation:** The current setting (1) is appropriate for discovery. For confirmation analyses where HERV-K background is undesirable, consider --outFilterMultimapNmax 3-5 as a sensitivity analysis.

### 5.4 Why BAM is Generated but Discarded

Despite generating a sorted BAM file (`--outSAMtype BAM SortedByCoordinate`), the pipeline does not publish this file. The BAM serves two purposes during execution:

1. **STAR internal processing:** STAR requires SAMtype specification to control its internal sort-and-write behavior. Specifying BAM SortedByCoordinate is the stable configuration validated to produce correct unmapped read output.
2. **Log.final.out generation:** STAR's alignment log (`{id}_Log.final.out`) records the number of input reads, uniquely mapped reads, multimappers, and unmapped reads. This log is the source of the RPM normalization denominator (see Section 11). The BAM is a byproduct of generating this log.

The BAM is not saved by default because: (a) it contains only human reads and thus has no downstream viral analysis value, and (b) storing 60M-read BAM files for each sample would rapidly exhaust the groups filesystem quota given `stageInMode = 'copy'` semantics at the SLURM work directory level.

### 5.5 Reference Genome: GRCh38

The pipeline uses the pre-built GRCh38 (hg38) STAR index at `/groups/tprice/pipelines/references/star_index`. This is the standard unmasked reference assembly.

**On the question of repeat-masking:** A repeat-masked reference (with RepeatMasker softmasking) could reduce false positive mapping of repetitive sequences including HERV elements. However, masking also reduces the efficiency of HERV-K read removal, making it less effective at removing HERV-K background before Kraken2. Given that HERV-K is a biological signal of interest in this study (neural tissue enrichment), the unmasked reference is the correct choice.

**On augmenting the STAR index with viral genomes:** An augmented reference that includes known latent herpesvirus genomes (HSV-1, VZV, CMV) as additional chromosomes would remove reads from those viruses before Kraken2, reducing their apparent abundance. This approach is appropriate when:
- The goal is to study a different viral population while subtracting known background
- A positive control virus has been spiked in and needs to be removed from the classification step
- The study focuses specifically on the non-latent virome (treating herpesviruses as biological background)

For studies where herpesvirus detection is the primary endpoint (e.g., HSV-1 in PD patients), the current unaugmented reference is correct.

### 5.6 Host Removal Efficiency Metric

The planned (but not yet implemented) host removal QC metric is the percentage of unmapped reads per sample. This is derivable from `Log.final.out` as:

```
pct_unmapped = (unmapped_reads / total_input_reads) * 100
```

For DRG bulk RNA-seq, expected unmapped fraction is typically 0.5-3% for healthy tissue samples. Values outside this range indicate:
- >5% unmapped: potential viral enrichment, RNA degradation, or non-human contamination
- <0.1% unmapped: very thorough host removal; may indicate index hopping issues or library from a low-complexity sample

This metric is planned for MultiQC injection in a future version.

## 6. Step 4: Kraken2 Classification

### 6.1 Overview and Algorithmic Basis

Kraken2 is a k-mer-based taxonomic classifier. Unlike alignment-based approaches (BLAST, minimap2), it does not perform sequence alignment. Instead, it:

1. Breaks each read into overlapping 35-mers (default k)
2. Looks up each k-mer in a hash table derived from the reference database
3. For each read, collects all taxon assignments from its k-mers
4. Assigns the read to the lowest common ancestor (LCA) of all k-mer hits
5. Applies the confidence filter (see 6.3) to decide whether to report the assignment

This approach achieves very high speed (~10-100x faster than alignment-based methods) at the cost of sensitivity to divergent sequences and specificity in regions of shared k-mer content between host and virus.

### 6.2 Command Parameters

```
kraken2 \
  --db {kraken2_db} \
  --threads {cpus} \
  --paired \
  --gzip-compressed \
  --confidence {kraken2_confidence} \
  --output {id}.kraken2.output \
  --report {id}.kraken2.report \
  {kraken2_extra_args} \
  {r1} {r2}
```

**--paired:** Both reads in the pair are processed together and their k-mer evidence is combined for a single LCA assignment. This is critical: treating paired reads independently would halve the k-mer evidence per assignment and reduce classification accuracy. The read boundary is marked by `|:|` in the k-mer string in the per-read output.

**--gzip-compressed:** Reads the compressed FASTQ directly without decompression to disk. Required for the STAR-output gzipped unmapped FASTQs.

Resource allocation: 16 CPUs, 64 GB memory (DB1; scales to 128 GB on OOM retry), 12-hour wall time. DB2 (PlusPF): 16 CPUs, 110 GB memory (scales to 220 GB), 12 hours. `stageInMode = 'symlink'` for both — database files must never be copied.

### 6.3 The Confidence Parameter: --confidence 0.1

The confidence parameter is the single most important tunable parameter in the entire pipeline. Its value determines what fraction of a read's k-mers must agree with the assigned taxon (and its ancestors) before the classification is reported. A confidence of 0.0 means any read with at least one k-mer match is classified; a confidence of 1.0 requires every k-mer to agree.

**Formula:** A classification at taxon T with confidence C is reported only if the fraction of k-mers supporting T (or any ancestor of T in the taxonomy tree) is ≥ C.

**--confidence 0.0:** Maximum sensitivity. Any read with a single k-mer match to a viral sequence is classified as viral. In DRG RNA-seq, this produces a very long tail of low-confidence viral hits driven by k-mer cross-matching between human transcripts and viral sequences sharing gene families (polymerases, kinases, capsid-like proteins). Not appropriate for discovery or quantification.

**--confidence 0.1 (default; all production runs):** At least 10% of k-mers in the read must agree with the LCA assignment. This is the standard setting recommended by the Kraken2 authors for general use. In practice for DRG RNA-seq with the viral-only database:
- HSV-1 in PD19 (46 reads) was detected at this setting — confirming it is sensitive enough to detect genuine signal at low abundance
- The noise floor from artifact taxa is manageable with the three-stage filter
- HERV-K (100% prevalence) is robustly detected with high read counts

**--confidence 0.05:** Would produce more classifications, potentially recovering divergent or low-coverage viral strains. Likely to increase HERV-K RPM and artifact taxa detection proportionally. Appropriate for a sensitivity analysis when a high-priority hit is being investigated and more reads are wanted for BLAST verification.

**--confidence 0.2-0.3:** Would reduce classifications. At 0.2, HSV-1 in PD19 might survive (46 reads at confidence 0.1 suggests reasonable k-mer agreement) but borderline hits near the 5-read threshold would be lost. Appropriate for a specificity validation analysis.

**--confidence 0.5:** High stringency. Appropriate when the database is known to be of high quality and the viral strains being sought are known and well-represented. Not appropriate for discovery of divergent or novel strains.

**Empirical grounding:** All empirical data in this pipeline was generated at --confidence 0.1. All findings (Tier 1=0 for non-PD cohort, HSV-1 in PD19, HERV-K RPM values) are specific to this setting. Changing this parameter requires revalidation of the entire filtering framework.

### 6.4 Database Architecture

**DB1: Viral-only database (~1.1 GB, Langmead Lab, AWS)**
Pre-built from RefSeq viral sequences. Contains complete viral genomes from RefSeq, indexed as Kraken2 database. Approximately 9,000-12,000 viral reference genomes. In competitive classification mode with this database, all non-viral k-mer hits produce no classification (they are simply absent from the database). This means a read from a human gene that shares some k-mers with a viral sequence will be classified as viral even if most of its k-mers are from the human genome — there is no human "competitor" to outcompete the viral assignment.

**DB2: PlusPF database (~87 GB, Langmead Lab)**
Includes all RefSeq complete genomes: bacteria, archaea, viruses, fungi, human, and protozoa. In competitive classification mode, a read must have a plurality of k-mers agreeing with a viral taxon in order to be classified as viral — human-derived k-mers compete against the viral assignment. This is a much more stringent test. A taxon detected in both DB1 and DB2 (Tier 1) has survived competitive classification against the full microbial and human genome space.

**Memory implications:** DB1 fits in 64 GB (the hash table is loaded into memory). DB2 requires 87+ GB for the hash.k2d file alone, necessitating 110 GB allocation with a 2x retry on OOM.

### 6.5 Per-Read Output Format

The `--output` file (`{id}.kraken2.output`) records one line per read with five tab-separated fields:

```
C/U  read_id  taxon_id  read_len  k-mer_string
```

- `C/U`: Classified or Unclassified
- `read_id`: FASTQ read identifier
- `taxon_id`: NCBI taxonomy ID of assignment (0 if unclassified)
- `read_len`: For paired reads, `len1|len2` (e.g., `150|148`)
- `k-mer_string`: Space-separated `taxon_id:count` tokens showing k-mer hit distribution, with `|:|` as the pair boundary marker

This file is the input to the BLAST verification offshoot's EXTRACT_KRAKEN2_READS module. It is not published by default (enable with `save_kraken2_output: true`) because it is very large (~1-3 GB per sample).

### 6.6 LCA Rollup and Its Implications

LCA rollup is the primary mechanism by which Kraken2 handles reads with k-mers matching multiple taxa. If a read has k-mers matching both HSV-1 (species) and VZV (species), both being herpesviruses, those k-mers are assigned to the LCA — which might be genus Simplexvirus or family Herpesviridae depending on the taxonomy. This causes read counts to accumulate at parent nodes rather than species nodes.

**Implications for DRG viromics:**
- A genuinely HSV-1-infected sample may show read counts distributed across HSV-1 (species), genus Simplexvirus, and family Herpesviridae nodes
- Bracken (Step 5) is specifically designed to redistribute these inflated parent counts back to species level
- Divergent viral strains with no close reference in the database will roll up to genus or family level and may never appear at species level regardless of Bracken redistribution

**Detecting rollup in practice:** In the Kraken2 report, if a family-level node shows high reads_direct but all child species show low reads_direct, LCA rollup is occurring. The KRAKEN2_FILTER script captures ranks `{S, S1, S2, G, G1, F}` for this reason — genus-level detections may represent species-level biology for taxa whose reference database representation is insufficient for species-level resolution.

### 6.7 k-mer Contamination Artifacts: Mechanism

Several documented artifacts arise from the k-mer approach:

**HERV-K (Human endogenous retrovirus K):** HERV-K sequences are integrated in the human genome and their loci are present in GRCh38. However, because `--outFilterMultimapNmax 1` in STAR means multi-mapping reads pass to Kraken2, reads from HERV-K loci that map to multiple positions in GRCh38 are classified by Kraken2 against the viral database and produce robust detections. HERV-K is genuine biological signal — these are endogenous retroviral transcripts from human genome loci. Prevalence: 100% across all cohorts, RPM ~31-42 depending on tissue type.

**CMV proxy (taxon 3050337, Cytomegalovirus papiinebeta3):** The Langmead viral DB contains baboon CMV (NC_055235.1) as a child of taxon 3050337 but no direct human CMV (HHV-5) Merlin genome at the species level humans recognize as HHV-5. Human CMV reads therefore assign to baboon CMV via LCA and roll up to the 3050337 node. The taxon_remap.tsv entry relabels this as "Human CMV (HHV-5) [proxy]" for report display. Detection at ~1 RPM background in most samples; Tier 2 only (absent from PlusPF competitive classification, which has the proper HHV-5 entry).

**Orthobunyaviruses (Shamonda, Schmallenberg):** These ruminant-tropic viruses share genus-level k-mers with common RNA-seq library prep reagents or with RNA-binding protein domains shared across bunyavirales. They are consistent top false positives in human RNA-seq and are in the artifact exclusion list.

## 7. Step 5: Bracken

### 7.1 Purpose: Redistributing LCA-Inflated Counts

Bracken (Bayesian Reestimation of Abundance after Classification with KrakEN) addresses a fundamental limitation of Kraken2's LCA approach. When k-mers from a read match multiple taxa in the database, Kraken2 assigns the read to the LCA node. This inflates read counts at genus and family levels and deflates them at species level, making raw Kraken2 reports unreliable for species-level quantification.

Bracken uses a Bayesian model trained on the database's k-mer distribution patterns to redistribute parent-node read counts back down to the most probable species-level assignment. It does this by:
1. For each species in the database, computing the expected k-mer distribution profile at the given read length
2. For reads assigned to parent nodes, redistributing them to child species in proportion to their expected k-mer profile similarity
3. Reporting adjusted species-level read counts

### 7.2 Command Parameters

```
bracken \
  -d {kraken2_db} \
  -i {kraken2_report} \
  -o {id}.bracken \
  -w {id}.bracken_report \
  -r {bracken_read_length} \
  -l {bracken_level} \
  -t {bracken_threshold}
```

| Parameter | Default | Description |
|---|---|---|
| -d | kraken2_db path | Must be the same database used for Kraken2 classification |
| -i | {id}.kraken2.report | Input Kraken2 report |
| -r | 150 | Read length in bp |
| -l | S | Taxonomic level (S = species) |
| -t | (bracken_threshold param) | Minimum k-mer count at the level below |

Resource allocation: 4 CPUs, 16 GB memory, 2-hour wall time. `stageInMode = 'symlink'` — shares the database with KRAKEN2_CLASSIFY.

### 7.3 The -r Parameter: Read Length Matching

**-r 150** must match the actual sequencing read length. This is not a tunable parameter — it is a database query. Bracken databases contain `.kmer_distrib` files indexed by read length (typically 50, 75, 100, 150, 200 bp). Specifying the wrong read length causes Bracken to use an inappropriate k-mer distribution model, producing incorrect abundance re-estimates.

**If read length varies within a cohort** (e.g., some samples trimmed to shorter mean lengths): Use the original library read length (150 bp) rather than the mean post-trimming length. The `.kmer_distrib` file represents the reference genome's k-mer profile at a given read length, not the actual library length. Using 150 is correct for all samples in this pipeline regardless of trimming.

**Database compatibility:** The `.kmer_distrib` files are specific to the database AND the read length. If the Kraken2 database is updated or rebuilt, Bracken must be re-run with `bracken-build` to regenerate the distribution files for the new database before any Bracken analyses.

### 7.4 Taxonomic Level: -l S (Species)

Species-level (S) is the default and correct choice for this application. Bracken can redistribute to any level: F (family), G (genus), S (species), or S1/S2 (subspecies).

**When to use genus-level (-l G):** Divergent viral strains with few reference sequences in the database may have most of their reads assigned at genus level by Kraken2, with insufficient k-mer data for species-level redistribution. In this case, forcing species-level analysis loses the signal. Genus-level analysis retains it. This is relevant for: novel herpesviruses, divergent retroviruses, and unclassified environmental viruses. Consider genus-level as a secondary analysis when species-level results show genus hits with no species-level detections.

### 7.5 Bracken Noise Amplification at Low Coverage

A critical caveat: Bracken can amplify noise when coverage is very low. If a parent node has 2-3 reads and Bracken redistributes them to 5-7 species based on k-mer profiles, each species ends up with 0-1 Bracken-assigned reads. These near-zero assignments are not biologically meaningful but would pass a min_reads=1 filter.

This is precisely why `min_reads=5` is the correct threshold after Bracken redistribution (not before). The empirical validation of min_reads=5 for this pipeline is described in Section 8.3.

### 7.6 Bracken Threshold (-t)

The Bracken threshold sets the minimum number of reads at the level one below the target level that must be present before redistribution is performed. If a genus node has fewer reads than the threshold, species-level redistribution does not occur for that genus. This prevents Bracken from redistributing very sparse genus-level signals into spurious species-level detections.

The current default value for `bracken_threshold` is set in the pipeline parameters. For the DRG viral database at the coverage levels observed (10-500 reads per detected species), the threshold is effectively permissive — most genuine detections have read counts well above any reasonable threshold.

## 8. Step 6: KRAKEN2_FILTER — Three-Stage Filtering

### 8.1 Architecture: Three Progressive Stages

The KRAKEN2_FILTER process invokes `bin/filter_kraken2_report.py` and produces five output files per sample, corresponding to three filtering stages plus diagnostic outputs:

| Output File | Stage | Description |
|---|---|---|
| `{id}.bracken_raw.tsv` | Stage 1 | All viral ranks (S, S1, S2, G, G1, F), no threshold applied |
| `{id}.minreads.tsv` | Stage 2 | After min_reads filter (default: reads ≥ 5), before artifact exclusion |
| `{id}.filtered.tsv` | Stage 3 | Final: min_reads + artifact exclusion |
| `{id}.filter_summary.tsv` | Diagnostic | Per-stage taxa count and read sum |
| `{id}.artifacts_removed.tsv` | Diagnostic | Taxa removed in Stage 3 with their read counts |

Viral ranks captured: `S` (species), `S1` (subspecies level 1), `S2` (subspecies level 2), `G` (genus), `G1` (genus level 1), `F` (family). This set is intentionally broad to catch both species-level detections and genuinely novel detections that resolve only to genus or family level.

### 8.2 Stage 1: bracken_raw

`bracken_raw` extracts all rows from the Bracken report with ranks in the viral rank set `{S, S1, S2, G, G1, F}` and taxon_id != 0. No read count threshold is applied. This represents the complete output of Bracken — all taxa that received any read assignment at any species-to-family level.

**Use case for bracken_raw:** Baseline analysis without any filtering; comparing filter stages; noise floor characterization. The `bracken_raw_matrix.tsv` is used in the filter funnel visualization in the HTML report to show how many taxa exist at each stage.

Taxon name remapping (from `taxon_remap.tsv`) is applied at Stage 1 and propagates through all subsequent stages — the remap is applied to the raw data, not just the final output.

### 8.3 Stage 2: minreads (default min_reads = 5)

The min_reads filter retains only taxa with `reads_direct >= min_reads`. The default value of 5 was validated empirically across the full cohort history:

**Empirical validation of min_reads = 5:**
- The noise floor across all samples in all cohorts is approximately 10 reads. In practice, no genuinely artifactual species-level detection has appeared in the 1-4 read range that was not already excluded by the artifact list or the dual-database comparison.
- Relaxing to min_reads = 1 adds zero new biological candidates in any run to date. It adds 30-80 new entries per sample, all of which are either artifact taxa or unresolvable taxonomy nodes, based on inspection of bracken_raw outputs.
- min_reads = 3 would be acceptable but provides no additional biological detections over min_reads = 5 while adding noise.
- The HSV-1 detection in PD19 (46 reads) is well above the 5-read threshold and is not affected by this parameter choice.

**When to change min_reads:**
- Decrease to 1-3: Ultra-low input samples with <10M total reads, or targeted detection scenarios where any k-mer evidence for a specific taxon is of interest (e.g., confirming presence/absence of a known pathogen). Not appropriate for discovery.
- Increase to 10-20: High-specificity screens where the goal is identifying only robustly detected taxa. Appropriate when sample quality is high, library depth is uniform, and the research question is prevalence-based rather than discovery-based.
- Increase to 50+: Conservative confirmation analysis. At 50 reads, only the most abundant viral detections survive. Appropriate for publications requiring a conservative threshold with explicit noise floor justification.

### 8.4 Stage 3: Artifact Exclusion

The artifact exclusion applies a blocklist of taxon IDs from `assets/artifact_taxa.tsv`. The current list contains 24 entries covering six artifact categories:

| Category | Example Taxa | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| ruminant_bug | Shamonda virus, Schmallenberg virus | Ruminant orthobunyaviruses; no human host range; top false positives in all human RNA-seq |
| insect_virus | Choristoneura fumiferana granulovirus, Betabaculovirus chofumiferanae | Baculoviruses; reagent contaminants from Illumina kit components; present at high RPM in all samples across all tissue types |
| phage | Ralstonia phage p12J / Porrectionivirus p12J, Salmonella phage SJ46 | Plant and enteric bacteriophages; environmental contamination |
| misclass | BeAn 58058 virus, Orthohantavirus oxbowense / Oxbow virus (660954) | Host reads misassigned at low confidence; confirmed cross-tissue uniform signal ruling out genuine infection |
| env_virus | Gihfavirus pelohabitans, Kinglevirus lutadaptatum | Environmental metagenome viruses; DRG-exclusive pattern from tissue-specific k-mer cross-mapping (confirmed in 5-muscle/6-DRG cohort: 0/5 muscle, 6/6 DRG, reads scale with library size) |
| avian_virus | Mardivirus columbidalpha1 | Avian herpesvirus; k-mer cross-mapping from human herpesvirus reads to conserved herpesvirus families |
| giant_virus | Pandoravirus neocaledonia | Acanthamoeba giant virus; eukaryotic HGT gene content causes k-mer cross-mapping with human transcripts |
| reagent | Sinsheimervirus phiX174 | Illumina PhiX sequencing control; presence indicates library failure or contamination |

**Adding entries to artifact_taxa.tsv:** Each entry requires:
1. `taxon_id`: NCBI taxonomy ID (integer)
2. `taxon_name`: Human-readable name for documentation
3. `reason`: Category tag and brief evidence statement

**ICTV reclassification caveat:** ICTV periodically renames and reassigns NCBI taxonomy IDs. When adding an entry, verify whether the current taxon ID has been superseded. The canonical example in this pipeline: Ralstonia phage p12J (247080) was reclassified as Porrectionivirus p12J (2956327). Both IDs are listed to catch the taxon regardless of which database version assigned it. Auditing the artifact list after Kraken2 database updates is mandatory.

**DRG-specific k-mer cross-mapping:** The Gihfavirus and Kinglevirus entries represent a tissue-type-specific artifact unique to DRG samples. DRG neurons express transcripts (neuronal ion channel genes, neuropeptide precursors, DRG-specific lncRNAs) with k-mer overlaps to these environmental metagenome sequences. The artifact was confirmed by the cohort comparison: 0 of 5 muscle samples detected these taxa; 6 of 6 DRG samples did, with read counts scaling with library size rather than showing a biological distribution. Any newly discovered "DRG-specific virus" without a plausible neurotropic biology should be examined for this pattern before biological interpretation.

### 8.5 Taxon Name Remapping

`assets/taxon_remap.tsv` maps taxon IDs to corrected display names for taxa whose NCBI/Kraken2 label is misleading in human tissue context. The remap is applied at Stage 1 (bracken_raw) and propagates to all outputs. The taxon_id is preserved unchanged for traceability.

Current entry: taxon 3050337 (*Cytomegalovirus papiinebeta3*) → `Human CMV (HHV-5) [proxy]`. This relabeling reflects the database imbalance described in Section 6.7: human CMV reads assign to the baboon CMV reference via LCA, so the display name should reflect the true biological identity while indicating the proxy nature of the assignment.

**When to add entries:** Only when there is high confidence that the NCBI label is systematically misleading for this tissue type, and when the corrected label is well-documented. The reason field is mandatory and should cite the evidence (e.g., the CMV taxonomy investigation document at `research/cmv_taxonomy_investigation.md`).

### 8.6 Three Output Matrices and When to Use Each

| Matrix | Contents | Primary Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| `viral_abundance_matrix.tsv` | Final filtered (min_reads + artifact exclusion) | All biological analysis; paper figures; statistical comparisons |
| `minreads_matrix.tsv` | After min_reads threshold only, before artifact exclusion | Reviewing artifact removal; diagnosing new artifacts; auditing what artifact exclusion removed |
| `bracken_raw_matrix.tsv` | All viral species from Bracken, no threshold | Noise floor analysis; filter stage visualization; assessing min_reads threshold choice |

The `viral_abundance_matrix.tsv` is the primary output. The other two matrices exist to make the filtering process transparent and auditable.

## 9. Step 7: Dual-Database Comparison and Tier System

### 9.1 Rationale for Dual-Database Comparison

The viral-only database classifies reads against viral references exclusively. Because there are no competing human or bacterial sequences in the database, reads from human genes with k-mer homology to viral sequences are classified as viral without competition. The PlusPF database includes the full microbial and human genome space. A read that classifies as viral in the viral-only database but as human or bacterial in PlusPF is almost certainly a false positive — the viral-only assignment was driven by k-mer overlap in the absence of a competing human reference.

The tier system encodes this logic.

### 9.2 Tier Definitions and Biological Interpretation

**Tier 1: Shared (detected in both DBs)**
A taxon detected in both the viral-only and PlusPF final filtered outputs. The detection survived competitive classification against the full microbial and human genome space. This is the highest confidence category.

*Biological interpretation:* Genuinely viral reads with k-mer profiles sufficiently distinct from human, bacterial, and archaeal sequences to survive competitive classification. Suitable for biological interpretation and reporting. Requires BLAST verification for any novel or unexpected Tier 1 detection before biological claims are made.

*Current empirical record:* Non-PD cohort (n=21): Tier 1 = 0. PD cohort (n=20): Tier 1 = 1 (HSV-1 in PD19, 46 reads, 1.89 RPM). HERV-K does not appear in Tier 1 — see below.

**Tier 2: Viral-only only (FP candidates)**
Detected in the viral-only database output but absent from PlusPF after artifact exclusion and min_reads filtering. These taxa were outcompeted in the PlusPF classification — their reads were claimed by human, bacterial, or other sequences in competitive mode.

*Biological interpretation:* Mostly false positives. However, some Tier 2 entries are genuine biological signals that are misclassified in PlusPF due to reference database imbalances:
- **HERV-K:** HERV-K reads are human-derived (endogenous retroviral elements in GRCh38). In the PlusPF database, these reads are correctly attributed to the human genome, not to exogenous retroviruses. In the viral-only database, they assign to HERV-K references. HERV-K is a genuine biological signal — endogenous retroviral transcription is real and tissue-relevant. It appears in Tier 2 because of the database competition, not because it is artifactual. **Do not add HERV-K to the artifact list.**
- **CMV proxy (taxon 3050337):** Absent from PlusPF in most samples because PlusPF's human CMV entry outcompetes the baboon CMV proxy. Tier 2 status is a database artifact; the underlying biology (CMV k-mer signal) may be real. BLAST verification is the appropriate next step for any CMV signal above 5 RPM.
- **Bona fide FPs:** Reagent contaminants (baculoviruses, orthobunyaviruses) typically persist in Tier 2 because they are not in the artifact list AND survive min_reads=5. This is exactly the purpose of Tier 2 — it is the inspection queue for adding new artifact entries.

**Tier 3: PlusPF only (warrants investigation)**
Detected in PlusPF but absent from the viral-only database final output. These are taxa that competitive classification with the full genome space resolves as viral, but the viral-only database does not detect.

*Biological interpretation:* Mostly noise from the competitive classification dynamics of PlusPF. Bacteria, fungi, and archaea with highly similar k-mers to viral sequences sometimes receive viral LCA assignments in PlusPF due to complex taxonomy. Genuine Tier 3 viral detections are rare but possible and warrant manual inspection of the db_comparison.tsv. A confirmed Tier 3 viral detection would suggest the viral-only database lacks a reference for that taxon.

### 9.3 Tier Comparison Outputs

| File | Contents |
|---|---|
| `db_comparison/db_comparison.tsv` | All taxa with tier classification and read counts from both DBs |
| `db_comparison/consensus_matrix.tsv` | Tier 1 taxa only; viral-only read columns (primary result for biological analysis) |
| `db_comparison/false_positive_candidates.tsv` | Tier 2 taxa; inspection list for new artifact entries |
| `db_comparison/db_comparison_summary.tsv` | Per-sample tier counts |
| `db_comparison/db_comparison.png` | Stacked bar chart embedded in HTML report |

### 9.4 HERV-K in Context

HERV-K biology in the DRG is a secondary finding of this pipeline but one of biological interest. Across all cohorts:

| Cohort | Mean RPM |
|---|---|
| Non-PD DRG | ~42 RPM |
| PD DRG | ~31.8 RPM |
| Muscle (controls) | ~29 RPM |

The DRG-enrichment of HERV-K expression (DRG > muscle) is consistent with known neuronal HERV-K transcriptional activity. The apparent reduction in PD DRG (~31.8 RPM vs. ~42 RPM non-PD DRG) is an observation requiring statistical validation with the full cohort before interpretation. HERV-K is always Tier 2 in dual-database mode. This is expected and correct.

## 10. BLAST Verification Offshoot

### 10.1 Purpose and Trigger Conditions

The BLAST verification offshoot (`workflows/blast_verification.nf`) provides alignment-based orthogonal validation for specific taxon detections identified by the primary k-mer pipeline. It is not run automatically as part of every pipeline execution. It is invoked post-hoc when:

1. A Tier 1 detection is found (mandatory for any biological claim)
2. A Tier 2 detection is being evaluated for artifact-vs-genuine classification
3. A novel or unexpected detection requires identity confirmation before the taxon is added to or removed from the artifact list

The canonical use case is the HSV-1 detection in PD19: 46 reads, 1.89 RPM, Tier 1 in the PD cohort.

### 10.2 Prerequisites: Saved Intermediate Files

The BLAST offshoot requires two intermediate outputs from the primary pipeline run that are not saved by default:

- `{id}.kraken2.output`: Per-read Kraken2 assignments (enable with `save_kraken2_output: true`)
- `{id}_unmapped_R1.fastq.gz` / `{id}_unmapped_R2.fastq.gz`: STAR-unmapped reads (enable with `save_unmapped_reads: true`)

For prospective validation runs (e.g., any future PD cohort run), both flags should be set in the config file. For retrospective validation of existing runs, the pipeline must be re-run with these flags.

The dedicated blast config at `assets/config_blast_pd19.yaml` and samplesheet at `assets/samplesheet_blast_pd19.csv` document the PD19 validation run.

### 10.3 Module 1: EXTRACT_KRAKEN2_READS

**Input:** `{id}.kraken2.output`, target taxon ID(s), host-removed FASTQ pair

**Operation:** Parses the per-read Kraken2 output file line by line. For each classified read:
- If `taxon_id` matches a target taxon ID exactly, the read ID is added to the extraction set
- If `--include-genus` is set, additionally scans the k-mer string for any token matching a target taxon ID (captures reads with supporting k-mers from the target taxon even if the LCA assignment was to a different node)

**Output:** Per-taxon read ID files (`{sample_id}.{taxon_id}.read_ids.txt`), a combined read ID file for seqtk, and extraction statistics TSV.

**seqtk extraction:** `seqtk subseq {r1} {combined_ids}` extracts matching read pairs. Output is gzipped FASTQ.

**--include-genus flag rationale:** For low-abundance detections near the noise floor (e.g., 46 HSV-1 reads), some reads may have LCA assignments at genus or family level (Simplexvirus, Alphaherpesviridae) rather than species level, if their k-mers match multiple herpesvirus species. The `--include-genus` flag recovers these reads by scanning the k-mer string for any HSV-1 taxon ID token. This increases sensitivity at the cost of specificity — reads captured by `--include-genus` but not by exact taxon match warrant separate reporting.

Resource allocation: 2 CPUs, 4 GB memory, 1-hour wall time.

### 10.4 Module 2: BLAST_VERIFY

**Input:** Extracted read pairs (gzipped FASTQ), local BLAST nt database

**Operation:**
1. Convert both read files to FASTA using `seqtk seq -a`
2. Optional subsampling via `seqtk sample -s 42` if `blast_max_reads` is set
3. Run `blastn` with tabular output (outfmt 6)

**BLAST command:**
```
blastn \
  -query query_all.fa \
  -db {blast_db_dir}/{blast_db_name} \
  -out {id}.{taxon_id}.blast_results.tsv \
  -outfmt "6 qseqid sseqid pident length mismatch gapopen qstart qend sstart send evalue bitscore" \
  -evalue {blast_evalue} \
  -max_target_seqs {blast_max_targets} \
  -word_size {blast_word_size} \
  -perc_identity {blast_perc_identity} \
  -num_threads {task.cpus}
```

**Parameters and their effects:**

| Parameter | Default | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| -evalue | 1e-5 | Maximum E-value for a hit to be reported. Standard threshold for significant sequence similarity |
| -max_target_seqs | 10 | Maximum subject sequences returned per query. Sufficient for identity confirmation; increase to 50 for diversity analysis |
| -word_size | 28 | Seed word size for initial alignment. Default for standard blastn. Reduce to 20-22 for divergent strains (HSV-1 strain diversity, ~95% identity expected) |
| -perc_identity | 80 | Minimum percentage identity for a hit to be reported. For human herpesviruses, expect >90% identity to reference strains; 80 is a permissive floor |
| blast_include_genus | false | Whether EXTRACT_KRAKEN2_READS uses the broader k-mer string search |

**-word_size guidance:** The default word_size=28 is appropriate for sequences with >95% identity to the BLAST database reference. For divergent viral strains (e.g., novel herpesviruses with ~70-80% identity), reducing word_size to 20-22 substantially increases sensitivity by allowing alignments to initiate on shorter exact matches. For the HSV-1 case (strain diversity ~95% identity), word_size=28 is correct.

Resource allocation: 8 CPUs, 32 GB memory, 4-hour wall time. `stageInMode = 'symlink'` — the BLAST nt database can exceed 100 GB.

### 10.5 Module 3: BLAST_ANALYZE

`bin/analyze_blast_results.py` performs two analyses:

**Identity Confirmation:**
- Parses outfmt 6 output, retaining best hit per query read (highest bitscore)
- Applies thresholds: pident ≥ 90%, evalue ≤ 1e-5 (configurable via `--pident-threshold`, `--evalue-threshold`)
- Confirmation criteria: ≥ 3 reads passing thresholds AND ≥ 50% of all BLASTed reads passing
- Reports: total reads BLASTed, reads passing threshold, mean % identity, mean alignment length, reads aligning to target reference accession

**Life Cycle Phase Inference (herpesviruses):**
Uses a curated gene catalog for HSV-1 (strain 17, NC_001806.2) mapping genomic coordinates to gene expression categories:

| Category | Genes | Biological Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| IE (Immediate-Early / alpha) | ICP0/RL2, ICP4/RS1, ICP27/UL54, ICP22/US1, ICP47/US12 | First transcription wave; expressed without prior viral protein synthesis; marker of lytic reactivation initiation |
| E (Early / beta) | TK/UL23, ICP8/UL29, DNApol/UL30, UL5, UL8, UL52, UL42, UL2, UL12 | Require IE gene products; precede viral DNA replication; DNA replication machinery |
| L (Late / gamma) | VP5/UL19, gB/UL27, gC/UL44, VP16/UL48, gD/US6, gI/US7, gE/US8 | Require viral DNA replication; structural proteins; virion assembly |
| LAT (Latency-associated transcript) | LAT_primary (coordinates 118848-128155) | Dominant transcript during latency; antisense to ICP0; 2 kb and 1.5 kb stable intron forms |

**Phase inference rules** (evaluated in order; first match wins):

| Required | Forbidden | Phase | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| {LAT} | {IE, E, L} | Latent | HIGH |
| {IE, E, L} | {} | Full lytic cycle | HIGH |
| {IE, E} | {L} | Early lytic | MEDIUM |
| {IE} | {E, L} | Reactivating (IE-only) | LOW |
| {L} | {IE, E} | Late lytic (IE-escaped) | LOW |
| {LAT, IE} | {} | Reactivating from latency | MEDIUM |

**Confidence downgrade:** If fewer than 5 reads map to annotated gene coordinates, confidence is forced to VERY LOW regardless of the rule match. At 46 total HSV-1 reads in PD19, the number mapping to annotated genes determines whether a reliable phase inference is possible.

**Output files:**
- `{id}.{taxon_id}.blast_summary.tsv`: Best hit per read with gene/category annotation
- `{id}.{taxon_id}.lifecycle_inference.tsv`: Phase call, confidence, evidence summary
- `{id}.{taxon_id}.lifecycle_report.html`: Human-readable HTML report with identity confirmation table, gene distribution table, phase inference, and top 20 BLAST hits

Resource allocation: 4 CPUs, 8 GB memory, 1-hour wall time.

### 10.6 minimap2 Coverage Analysis

The design specification includes optional minimap2 alignment of extracted reads to the HSV-1 reference genome for breadth-of-coverage analysis. This provides complementary evidence to BLAST:

- BLAST confirms identity (per-read similarity)
- minimap2 coverage breadth indicates genome-wide distribution (ruling out k-mer contamination from a single locus)

For a genuine latent HSV-1 infection in DRG, expected coverage would be:
- LAT locus: high coverage (LAT is the dominant transcript in latency)
- Immediate-early genes: low-to-absent coverage (silenced in latency)
- Late genes: absent (not expressed in latency)

For lytic reactivation:
- Broad genome coverage
- IE and E genes well-represented
- L genes may be lower if reactivation is early stage

### 10.7 PD19 HSV-1 Validation: Use Case

The PD19 HSV-1 detection (46 reads, 1.89 RPM, Tier 1) is the first biologically interpretable viral detection in the program. The BLAST verification workflow was designed specifically to validate this finding. The expected validation procedure:

1. Confirm taxon identity: ≥ 23 of 46 reads (≥50%) BLAST to HSV-1 references (NC_001806.2 or related strains) with ≥ 90% identity and E-value ≤ 1e-5
2. Perform life cycle phase inference: Determine whether reads map to LAT (latent) vs. IE/E/L genes (lytic)
3. Assess coverage breadth: minimap2 to NC_001806.2; breadth >5% of genome would be significant at 46 reads
4. Report confidence: BLAST_ANALYZE produces an HTML report with all metrics

At 46 reads, all phase inferences will carry VERY LOW or LOW confidence due to the sparse annotated read count. This is the correct statistical behavior — 46 reads is insufficient for reliable phase inference. The identity confirmation, however, is achievable: if 25+ of 46 reads BLAST to HSV-1 with >90% identity, the detection is orthogonally confirmed.

## 11. RPM Normalization

### 11.1 Denominator: STAR Input Reads

RPM (reads per million) is computed as:

```
RPM = (reads_direct / total_STAR_input_reads) × 1,000,000
```

The denominator is extracted from `STAR_Log.final.out`:
```
"Number of input reads" → parsed by aggregate_virome.py (parse_star_input_reads function)
```

This value is the number of trimmed read pairs entering STAR — i.e., the total library size after adapter trimming and quality filtering. This is the correct denominator for cross-sample normalization because it represents the total sequencing effort applied to the sample.

### 11.2 Rationale for Using STAR Input Reads vs. Unmapped Reads

Alternative RPM normalization strategies:

**Unmapped reads as denominator:** Would normalize by the number of reads reaching Kraken2. This would amplify signals from samples with high host removal efficiency (fewer unmapped reads = higher RPM per viral read) and suppress signals from samples with low efficiency. This is mathematically unstable for samples with very different host removal rates and creates a spurious correlation between host removal efficiency and apparent viral abundance.

**Total trimmed reads (STAR input) as denominator (current):** Normalizes by library size. Assumes that variation in host removal efficiency is noise to be controlled for, not a variable to amplify. Consistent cross-sample comparison requires a common denominator that does not depend on the step under analysis.

**Raw reads as denominator:** Would conflate trimming efficiency with viral abundance. Not appropriate.

### 11.3 Limitations

1. **Different host removal rates:** If two samples have different percentages of host-derived reads (e.g., one sample has 98% human reads, another has 95%), the same absolute number of viral reads will yield different RPM values. This is the intended behavior — RPM reflects the fraction of the total library attributable to viral RNA — but it means that samples with higher host transcript abundance appear to have lower viral RPM even with the same number of viral reads.

2. **Library size variation:** Samples with very different total read counts (e.g., 30M vs. 120M) have different detection sensitivities. RPM normalization does not correct for this — it is a rate normalization, not a power normalization. Samples with very low read counts may have higher RPM variance due to Poisson counting noise.

3. **No adjustment for Bracken redistribution:** RPM is computed from Bracken-adjusted read counts, which are fractional re-estimated values. Interpretation of RPM for Bracken-redistributed taxa should account for the fact that the read count is a statistical estimate, not a direct observation.

## 12. Report Generation

### 12.1 HTML Report (virome_report/summary.html)

The report is generated by `bin/virome_report.py` and is the primary deliverable for each pipeline run. It integrates all three abundance matrices and the filter summary data.

**Diversity Table:**
Per-sample metrics:
- Richness: Number of unique taxa in the final filtered matrix
- Shannon diversity index (H' = -Σ(pᵢ × ln pᵢ), where pᵢ is RPM fraction per taxon)
- Total viral reads: Sum of reads across all taxa in the filtered matrix

Diversity metrics are computed from the final filtered matrix only (not bracken_raw or minreads). This means they reflect the virome after artifact removal and minimum read thresholding.

**Filtering Funnel Chart:**
Per-sample bar chart showing taxa count at each of the three filter stages (bracken_raw, minreads, filtered). Visualizes how many taxa are removed at each step. A sample with a very high bracken_raw count but low filtered count indicates either many low-abundance detections (min_reads filter is doing substantial work) or many artifact taxa (artifact exclusion is doing substantial work). The filter_summary.tsv per-stage read sums enable this distinction.

**Read Attrition Chart:**
Per-sample bar chart showing total reads retained at each filter stage. Complements the taxa funnel — it is possible to remove many taxa but retain most reads if the removed taxa are low-abundance artifacts. High read loss at the min_reads stage (not taxa loss) would indicate that many reads are distributed across many low-abundance taxa — a pattern inconsistent with a specific viral infection.

**Top-N Abundance Heatmap:**
Rows = top N taxa by total RPM across all samples; columns = samples; color = log2(RPM + 1). Hierarchical clustering of both rows and columns is applied. HERV-K will dominate this heatmap in all current cohorts given its 31-42 RPM range vs. other taxa at 0-5 RPM.

**Prevalence Bar Chart:**
Number of samples in which each taxon is detected (final filtered matrix). HERV-K: 100% prevalence. HSV-1 (PD cohort): 1/20 samples. Taxa with 100% prevalence and consistent RPM across samples are candidate artifacts; taxa with low prevalence and high RPM in specific samples are candidate genuine infections.

**Dual-DB Comparison Plot:**
Present only when `kraken2_db2` is set. Stacked bar chart: x-axis = samples, y-axis = taxa count; bars stacked by Tier 1 (green), Tier 2 (orange), Tier 3 (purple). Shows at a glance whether specific samples have unusual Tier 1 counts.

### 12.2 MultiQC Report (multiqc_report.html)

Aggregates: FastQC (raw reads), Trimmomatic trim logs, STAR alignment statistics, Kraken2 classification reports, Bracken outputs. Standard MultiQC visualizations apply. Data directory: `multiqc_report_data/` (not `multiqc_data/` — the `--filename` flag changes the data directory naming convention).

Key MultiQC metrics for viromics QC:
- Trimmomatic: percent reads surviving, percent reads dropped — outliers indicate library quality issues
- STAR: percent uniquely mapped — high values (>85%) indicate good host depletion efficiency; unexpectedly low values may indicate RNA quality issues
- Kraken2: percent classified — should be low for host-depleted reads against viral-only DB (typically 0.5-5%); very high classified percentage would indicate either viral enrichment or a database/parameter issue

## 13. HPC and System Parameters

### 13.1 stageInMode Decisions

Nextflow's `stageInMode` controls how input files are made available to each process in the work directory. The default `stageInMode = 'copy'` is set globally in `conf/base.config`. Several processes override this to `'symlink'`:

| Process | stageInMode | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Default | copy | Required for Apptainer with --cleanenv, which cannot follow symlinks across filesystem boundaries |
| STAR_HOST_REMOVAL | symlink | 33 GB STAR index; copying would exhaust groups quota and take >10 minutes per sample |
| KRAKEN2_CLASSIFY | symlink | Viral-only DB ~1.1 GB; shared across all samples running concurrently; symlink + autoMounts |
| KRAKEN2_CLASSIFY_DB2 | symlink | PlusPF DB ~87 GB; must never be copied |
| BRACKEN | symlink | Shares DB with KRAKEN2_CLASSIFY |
| BRACKEN_DB2 | symlink | Shares DB with KRAKEN2_CLASSIFY_DB2 |
| BLAST_VERIFY | symlink | BLAST nt database can exceed 100 GB |

**Why copy is the default:** Apptainer with `--cleanenv` does not inherit the host filesystem mounts. When SLURM stages a symlink into the work directory and Apptainer is invoked, the symlink target may not be accessible from inside the container if it points to a different filesystem. `stageInMode = 'copy'` creates a real file copy inside the work directory, guaranteeing container visibility. The large database exceptions use `symlink` because the databases reside on the groups filesystem, which is mounted inside Apptainer via the SLURM job configuration.

### 13.2 Memory Scaling on OOM

KRAKEN2_CLASSIFY and KRAKEN2_CLASSIFY_DB2 use closure-based memory scaling:
```groovy
memory = { 64.GB * task.attempt }   // DB1: 64 GB → 128 GB on retry
memory = { 110.GB * task.attempt }  // DB2: 110 GB → 220 GB on retry
```

Error codes triggering retry: 143 (SIGTERM from SLURM), 137 (OOM kill), 104, 134, 139. MaxRetries = 2. If a Kraken2 job OOMs twice, the pipeline reports the failure rather than endlessly scaling.

### 13.3 Login Node vs. Compute Node

**Never run Nextflow from the Juno login node.** Login nodes have shared memory that is unpredictably exhausted by concurrent users. The Nextflow JVM requires at least 512 MB - 2 GB heap to manage concurrent job tracking. On a login node, even `nextflow run` without any compute will fail with `Cannot allocate memory` when the login node is under load.

Correct procedure:
```bash
# Step 1: get an interactive compute node
srun --account=tprice --partition=normal --cpus-per-task=2 --mem=4G --time=4:00:00 --pty bash

# Step 2: set JVM heap limits and launch
export NXF_JVM_ARGS="-Xms512m -Xmx2g"
nextflow run main.nf -profile slurm -params-file assets/config.yaml
```

`NXF_JVM_ARGS="-Xms512m -Xmx2g"` sets: minimum heap 512 MB (prevents JVM from starting with 32 MB and thrashing), maximum heap 2 GB (sufficient for tracking 15-20 concurrent SLURM jobs with Nextflow's process graph).

### 13.4 Work Directory

The `slurm` profile sets `workDir = /scratch/juno/$USER/nf_work`. The scratch filesystem has high-bandwidth storage appropriate for large intermediate files (STAR BAMs, Kraken2 outputs). The groups filesystem (`/groups/tprice/`) has limited quota and must not be used as a work directory when running with `stageInMode = 'copy'` on samples with large inputs (STAR BAM files for 60M-read samples can exceed 20 GB per sample).

## 15. Decision Guide: Scenario-Based Parameter Guidance

### 15.1 Scenario: "I want to find divergent viral strains not well-represented in the database"

These strains will have many k-mers rolling up to genus or family level rather than species, and the confidence parameter may suppress their classification entirely.

**Parameter adjustments:**
- `kraken2_confidence: 0.05` — Reduced from default 0.1; captures reads with lower k-mer agreement (appropriate for divergent strains where fewer k-mers match the closest reference)
- `bracken_level: G` — Genus-level Bracken; preserves genus-level detections that don't redistribute to species
- `min_reads: 3` — Slightly relaxed threshold to catch weaker signals
- `blast_word_size: 20` — Reduced for BLAST verification; necessary for aligning reads from strains with ~70-80% identity to reference

**Post-analysis:** Inspect the bracken_raw matrix for genus-level detections with no corresponding species-level hits. These are candidate divergent strains. BLAST verification is mandatory before any biological interpretation.

**Risk:** Higher false positive rate. Every candidate must be BLAST-verified and reviewed against the artifact list before reporting.

### 15.2 Scenario: "I want maximum specificity for publication confirmation"

Confirming a known detection (e.g., HSV-1 in PD19) with the most stringent pipeline settings.

**Parameter adjustments:**
- `kraken2_confidence: 0.3` — Higher confidence threshold; retains only reads with strong k-mer agreement
- `min_reads: 10` — Stricter minimum; eliminates any borderline signals
- `bracken_level: S` — Species level; do not relax to genus for a known species-level target
- `save_kraken2_output: true` and `save_unmapped_reads: true` — Required for BLAST verification
- Run BLAST verification on all Tier 1 detections with `blast_perc_identity: 90`

**Interpretation:** A detection surviving confidence=0.3 + min_reads=10 + dual-database tiering + BLAST verification ≥ 90% identity is a publication-quality finding.

**Risk:** May miss genuine low-abundance infections. HSV-1 at 46 reads should survive confidence=0.3 if the k-mer agreement is strong, but this must be verified.

### 15.3 Scenario: "I have ultra-low input samples with <10M total reads"

Low-input samples have reduced detection power. Every biological read is precious.

**Parameter adjustments:**
- `min_reads: 1-3` — Relaxed threshold; Poisson noise floor is lower at low total read counts
- `trim_minlen: 20-25` — Retain shorter reads from fragmented RNA
- `kraken2_confidence: 0.05` — Relaxed confidence; fewer k-mers available per read
- Do NOT activate dual-DB mode — PlusPF competitive classification requires sufficient read depth to distinguish viral from human competitive assignments; underpowered samples will have most viral reads outcompeted

**Post-analysis:** All detections require manual inspection. The filter funnel will show very few reads at every stage; a detection with even 2-3 reads in a low-input sample may represent a larger biological signal.

**Risk:** Very high false positive rate. This mode should only be used for targeted detection of specific, high-priority pathogens where absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

### 15.4 Scenario: "I am building a reference virome atlas for a new tissue type"

Starting a new cohort from a new tissue (spinal cord, sympathetic ganglia, satellite glial cells).

**Parameter adjustments (initial discovery run):**
- All default parameters
- `save_kraken2_output: true` — Required for any BLAST verification
- `save_unmapped_reads: true` — Required for any BLAST verification

**Artifact list protocol:**
1. Run with the existing `artifact_taxa.tsv` as a starting point
2. Examine the `minreads_matrix.tsv` vs. `viral_abundance_matrix.tsv` to see what artifact exclusion removed
3. Examine the `false_positive_candidates.tsv` (Tier 2) output carefully
4. For each new consistent detection across all samples (>50% prevalence): is it in the artifact list? If not, is it biologically plausible for the new tissue type?
5. DRG-specific artifacts (Gihfavirus, Kinglevirus) should be reviewed: do they appear in the new tissue? If not, they are correctly excluded. If they do appear at the same prevalence pattern, expand the artifact list.
6. Document the provenance of every new artifact list entry

**Taxon remap protocol:**
- Review all 100% prevalence Tier 2 detections for database imbalance
- If a detection shows consistent RPM across all samples and BLAST reveals a closer human-tropic relative not in the viral-only database, add a remap entry

**ICTV audit:** After any Kraken2 database update, run the new database against the existing artifact list. Check whether any artifact taxon IDs have been superseded by ICTV reclassification. Generate a summary of artifact list entries that are no longer in the database taxonomy tree.

### 15.5 Scenario: "I have a new disease cohort and need to validate the DRG-specific artifact entries"

The DRG-specific artifacts (Gihfavirus, Kinglevirus) were validated in a 5-muscle/6-DRG cohort. A new disease cohort requires reconfirmation that these patterns hold.

**Validation procedure:**
1. Run both disease samples and matched tissue controls (if available) through the pipeline with `artifact_list: null` (artifact exclusion disabled) and `min_reads: 1` to see the complete raw signal
2. For each DRG-specific artifact candidate: plot reads per sample for disease vs. control DRG, and vs. any available non-DRG tissue
3. Expected pattern for confirmed artifact: reads scale with library size, no difference between disease and control, DRG >> non-DRG
4. Unexpected pattern warranting investigation: reads elevated specifically in disease samples and not in controls — this is a potential biological signal even if the taxon is in the artifact list

**Parameter for validation run:**
```yaml
artifact_list: null
taxon_remap: null
min_reads: 1
save_kraken2_output: true
```

This generates the `bracken_raw_matrix.tsv` as the complete, unfiltered output for manual audit.

### 15.6 Scenario: "I want to characterize HERV-K biology specifically"

HERV-K is always Tier 2 in dual-database mode and is not in the artifact list. It is a genuine biological signal from endogenous retroviral transcription.

**Analysis recommendations:**
- Use `bracken_level: S` (HERV-K detects reliably at species level)
- Use `kraken2_confidence: 0.1` (default; HERV-K has strong k-mer agreement)
- Note: `--outFilterMultimapNmax 1` passes HERV-K reads to Kraken2 (multi-mapping reads from integrated proviral loci)
- For quantitative comparison: use RPM from `viral_abundance_matrix.tsv`; the RPM values (~31-42 RPM depending on tissue type) are reliable for differential analysis
- Statistical comparison of HERV-K between PD and non-PD DRG requires accounting for RNA quality (RIN) and total library size as covariates — HERV-K RPM in degraded samples may be elevated due to preferential survival of repetitive element reads relative to longer coding transcripts
- Reducing `--outFilterMultimapNmax` to 3-5 in STAR would reduce HERV-K read pass-through and may be informative as a sensitivity analysis

### 15.7 Scenario: "I want to track a specific pathogen across a longitudinal cohort"

Monitoring a known viral target (e.g., HSV-1 in a patient with recurring DRG pain) across multiple timepoints.

**Parameter adjustments:**
- `save_kraken2_output: true` and `save_unmapped_reads: true` — Required for longitudinal BLAST verification
- `kraken2_confidence: 0.1` — Keep default; changing confidence between timepoints introduces systematic bias
- `min_reads: 5` — Keep default; changing threshold between timepoints makes abundance comparisons invalid
- After each timepoint run, run BLAST_VERIFY on the target taxon to track phase changes over time

**Longitudinal interpretation:**
- RPM change between timepoints: indicates change in viral RNA abundance
- Phase change (LAT → IE → full lytic) across timepoints: indicates reactivation sequence
- Loss of detection below min_reads=5: does not rule out viral presence; it means <5 reads were recovered, which could reflect very deep latency or RNA quality variation
